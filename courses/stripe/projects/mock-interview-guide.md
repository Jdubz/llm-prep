# Mock Interview Guide — For the Friend Helping You Practice

Run these two mock interviews back-to-back with a break in between,
just like the real schedule. Use a timer. Be strict.

---

## Mock Interview 1: Experience & Goals (Ali's Round)

**Duration: 45 minutes** (real is 60 min, but 45 is enough for practice)

### Setup for the mock interviewer

You are evaluating: career fit, communication, and technical depth.
You want to understand what this person actually DID (not the team),
what was hard about it, and what they learned. Push back on vague answers.

### Prompts (ask in this order)

**Opening (5 min):**
1. "Tell me about yourself and what you're looking for in your next role."

**Project Deep Dive (25 min):**
Pick ONE of these, then drill into it for 25 minutes:

2. "Tell me about the most technically challenging project you've worked on."

Follow-up questions (ask ALL of these during the deep dive):
- "What was YOUR specific role vs the team?"
- "Walk me through the technical architecture."
- "What was the hardest technical decision you made? What alternatives did you consider?"
- "What constraints were you working under?"
- "What did you sacrifice and why? What was the trade-off?"
- "What broke or went wrong? How did you handle it?"
- "What was the measurable outcome?"
- "What would you do differently if you did it again?"
- "How did this project enable other people to be successful?"

**Career & Motivation (10 min):**
3. "Why Stripe? Why TSE specifically — not SWE, not product, not sales?"
4. "What does a great day at work look like for you?"
5. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a team decision. What happened?"

**Candidate Questions (5 min):**
6. "What questions do you have for me?"

### Rubric — Score each 1-4

| Signal | 1 (Weak) | 2 (OK) | 3 (Good) | 4 (Strong) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Owns their contribution** | Says "we" for everything, can't distinguish their work from the team's | Mentions their role but stays vague ("I helped with...") | Clearly states what they did vs the team | Specific actions with quantified impact ("I implemented X, which reduced Y by Z%") |
| **Technical depth** | Can't explain architecture decisions when pressed | Explains what was built but not WHY | Explains decisions with trade-offs and alternatives considered | Demonstrates deep understanding — can discuss what would break, scale limits, edge cases |
| **Handles follow-ups** | Gets flustered or contradicts earlier statements | Answers adequately but doesn't add new information | Adds meaningful detail on follow-up that strengthens the story | Follow-ups reveal deeper insight — the story gets BETTER under pressure |
| **Lessons learned** | "I wouldn't change anything" or generic platitudes | Identifies something they'd change but can't articulate why | Clear lesson with specific alternative approach | Lesson connects to a principle they now apply broadly ("this taught me to always...") |
| **Why Stripe/TSE** | Generic ("great company", "good culture") | Shows some research but reasons could apply to any company | Specific to Stripe + TSE with personal connection | Connects personal experience to the exact role — "I've been the developer debugging at 11pm, I want to be on the other side" |
| **Communication** | Rambles, hard to follow, doesn't answer the question asked | Answers the question but needs prompting to stay focused | Clear structure, appropriate detail level, answers directly | Concise, well-structured, signals when changing topics, asks clarifying questions when appropriate |

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Story is longer than 3 minutes before the interviewer can ask a follow-up
- Can't name a specific technical decision — only describes what was built
- Says "we" exclusively — never "I"
- "Why Stripe" answer could apply to any tech company
- Gets defensive when asked "what would you do differently"
- No questions for the interviewer

## Mock Interview 2: Users First & Curious (Tyler's Round)

**Duration: 35 minutes** (real is 45 min)

### Setup for the mock interviewer

You are evaluating TWO specific Stripe operating principles:
1. **Users First** — Does this person think about the end user before the architecture?
2. **Curious** — Does this person dig into things, learn proactively, change their mind?

IMPORTANT: These stories should have ZERO overlap with the stories
told in the Experience & Goals round. If they reuse a story, call it out.

### Prompts (ask 4-5 of these, ~7 min each)

1. "Tell me about a time you designed a solution starting from the user's perspective rather than the technical architecture."

2. "Tell me about a time you proactively identified a UX or developer experience issue that nobody asked you to fix."

3. "Tell me about a time you went deep on something out of pure curiosity — not because anyone asked you to."

4. "Tell me about a time you changed your approach based on new information. What made you willing to change course?"

5. "Tell me about a time you had to balance what the user wanted against what was technically feasible. How did you figure out what they actually needed?"

6. "Describe a time you learned something unexpected from a user or stakeholder interaction."

**Follow-up questions to use on ANY story:**
- "What surprised you about the user's perspective?"
- "How did you validate that your solution actually solved their problem?"
- "What assumption did you have going in that turned out to be wrong?"
- "What did you learn from this that you still carry today?"
- "If a teammate proposed the original (wrong) approach, how would you convince them?"

### Rubric — Score each 1-4

| Signal | 1 (Weak) | 2 (OK) | 3 (Good) | 4 (Strong) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Users First** | Story is about technology, user is an afterthought | Mentions the user but the decision was really driven by technical preference | User's need drove the design decision — can articulate what the user needed and why | Went TO the user, discovered something non-obvious, changed the approach based on what they learned |
| **Proactive** | Was assigned the work | Noticed the problem but waited to be asked | Noticed the problem and acted on it without being asked | Identified a problem nobody else saw, self-assigned the fix, and it had measurable impact |
| **Curiosity** | Can't point to a time they dug in beyond requirements | Learned something new but only because the project required it | Went beyond what was needed out of genuine interest | The curiosity led somewhere unexpected — discovered hidden value, changed team practices, or opened a new direction |
| **Adaptability** | "My original approach was right" | Changed approach but can't articulate why the new one was better | Clear before/after — old approach, new information, new approach, better outcome | Shows intellectual humility — names the wrong assumption, explains what changed their mind, demonstrates growth |
| **Concrete results** | No outcome mentioned | Outcome is qualitative ("people liked it") | Outcome has a number or specific feedback | Outcome is quantified AND had second-order effects ("the widget became the basis for all future dashboards") |
| **Story overlap** | Retells the same story from Ali's round | Different story but hits the same themes | Completely different story and theme | Stories complement each other — together they paint a fuller picture of the candidate |

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Every story starts with "I was asked to..." (not proactive)
- Can't name what the USER needed — only what the SYSTEM needed
- Curiosity story is actually just doing their job
- "Changed approach" story doesn't name the original assumption that was wrong
- Stories overlap with the Experience & Goals round
- Can't answer "what surprised you about the user" — indicates they didn't actually talk to users

## After Both Rounds

### Debrief Questions (discuss together, 10 min)

1. Which story was the strongest? Why?
2. Which story fell apart under follow-up questions?
3. Were there any stories that overlapped between the two rounds?
4. Did any answer feel rehearsed vs authentic?
5. Was there a moment where the candidate got defensive instead of reflective?
6. Could the candidate name specific things THEY did (not the team)?
7. Overall: would you want to work with this person?

### Scoring Summary

- **12-16 per round:** Not ready — needs more story development
- **17-20 per round:** Borderline — stories are there but lack depth or specificity
- **21-24 per round:** Strong — clear stories with depth, good under follow-ups
